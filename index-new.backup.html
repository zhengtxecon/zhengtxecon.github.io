Evidence-Based Research for Inclusive Labour Markets in the Age of Automation

I study how technological change reshapes firms, workers, and policy in emerging economies. My work bridges computational social science, labour economics, and development economics to help decision-makers build resilient, human-centered labour markets.

Labour Market Dynamics

Analyzing worker mobility and wage inequality in transitional economies through administrative data.

Automation & Policy

Measuring the causal impact of AI adoption on firm productivity and employment structure.

Development Economics

Bridging the gap between technological frontier and developing labor institutions.

Computational Methods

Developing novel structural models to simulate policy outcomes in complex adaptive systems.

Research Agenda.

My research combines rigorous econometric identification with structural modeling to provide actionable insights for policymakers.

View Methodology
01

DATA COLLECTION

Leveraging administrative micro-data and large-scale surveys.

Constructing comprehensive panel datasets to track individual worker trajectories across decades.

  • Matched employer-employee data
  • Web-scraped job vacancy data
  • Satellite imagery for regional development
02

CAUSAL INFERENCE

Isolating the effects of technological shocks.

Utilizing natural experiments and instrumental variables to identify causal mechanisms driving inequality.

  • Difference-in-differences designs
  • Regression discontinuity
  • Shift-share instruments
03

STRUCTURAL MODELING

Micro-founding macroeconomic trends.

Building equilibrium models to understand general equilibrium effects that reduced-form methods miss.

  • Heterogeneous agent models
  • Search and matching frictions
  • Task-based production functions
04

POLICY SIMULATION

From theory to practice.

Using estimated parameters to simulate counterfactual policy scenarios for government advisories.

  • Minimum wage effects
  • Retraining subsidy optimization
  • Taxation of robots/automation
